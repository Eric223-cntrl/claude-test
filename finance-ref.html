Income Statement (P&L)
Also called the Profit & Loss Statement or Statement of Operations
What Is the Income Statement?

The income statement reports a company's financial performance over a specific period — typically a quarter or fiscal year. It answers the fundamental question: Did the company make money?

It flows top-down from Revenue (total sales) through a series of expenses and deductions to arrive at Net Income — the "bottom line." Every subtotal along the way (Gross Profit, EBITDA, EBIT) provides a different lens on profitability.

Unlike the balance sheet (a snapshot) or cash flow statement (cash movements), the income statement reflects accrual accounting — revenue and expenses are recognized when earned or incurred, not necessarily when cash changes hands.

Revenue − Expenses = Net Income
Key distinction: Net Income ≠ Cash Flow. A company can show strong net income yet run out of cash due to timing differences, non-cash items, and working capital changes.
Structure at a Glance
1
Revenue — Total sales/service income before any deductions
2
− Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) — Direct costs to produce goods or services
3
= Gross Profit — Revenue remaining after direct costs
4
− Operating Expenses — SG&A, R&D, D&A (overhead & infrastructure)
5
= EBIT / Operating Income — Profit from core operations before financing costs
6
± Interest & Other — Cost of debt, investment income, non-operating items
7
= EBT — Earnings before income tax
8
− Income Tax Expense — Federal, state, and local taxes
9
= Net Income — The "bottom line" — profit attributable to shareholders

Balance Sheet
Also called the Statement of Financial Position
What Is the Balance Sheet?

The balance sheet is a point-in-time snapshot of what a company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the net residual interest of shareholders (equity). It is produced as of a specific date — typically the last day of a reporting period.

It is governed by a single inviolable equation. If the two sides don't balance, there is an accounting error:

Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

Assets are listed in order of liquidity (most liquid first). Liabilities are listed in order of due date (soonest due first). The balance sheet links to both the income statement (via retained earnings) and the cash flow statement (via changes in cash).

The three-statement link: Net Income from the P&L flows into Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet. The ending cash balance on the Cash Flow Statement equals Cash on the Balance Sheet.
Structure at a Glance
ASSETS
CA
Current Assets — Convertible to cash within 12 months (Cash, AR, Inventory)
LTA
Long-Term Assets — PP&E, Intangibles, Goodwill — held >12 months
LIABILITIES + EQUITY
CL
Current Liabilities — Due within 12 months (AP, Accrued, Current Debt)
LTL
Long-Term Liabilities — Debt and obligations due beyond 12 months
EQ
Shareholders' Equity — Residual after all liabilities (Common Stock + Retained Earnings)

Cash Flow Statement
Statement of Cash Flows — tracks cash inflows and outflows over a period
What Is the Cash Flow Statement?

The cash flow statement reconciles the difference between net income (accrual-based) and actual cash generated or consumed. It is divided into three sections reflecting the three fundamental activities of any business.

It answers: Where did our cash come from, and where did it go? A company may be profitable yet cash-poor (if customers pay slowly) or cash-rich yet unprofitable (if it's drawing down capital).

Accounting Alert: Under the indirect method (most common in the US), Operating Activities starts with Net Income and adjusts for non-cash items and working capital changes. This is not the same as collecting all cash receipts and subtracting all cash payments.
Three Sections
1
Operating Activities (OCF) — Cash generated from the core business. Starts with Net Income, adds back non-cash charges (D&A), adjusts for working capital changes. This is the most important section — sustainable businesses must generate positive OCF.
2
Investing Activities (ICF) — Cash spent on or received from long-term investments. CapEx (buying PP&E) is a large outflow here. Asset sales, acquisitions, and divestitures also appear. Typically negative for growing companies.
3
Financing Activities (FCF) — Cash flows related to raising and returning capital. Includes debt issuance/repayment, equity issuance, share buybacks, and dividends. Note: "Free Cash Flow" (a key metric) is not this section — it's OCF − CapEx.

Statement of Stockholders' Equity
Also called the Statement of Changes in Equity
What Is the Statement of Stockholders' Equity?

This statement explains every change in the equity section of the balance sheet from the beginning to the end of a reporting period. It serves as the bridge connecting the income statement to the balance sheet via retained earnings.

Equity increases when a company earns net income or issues stock. Equity decreases when it pays dividends, repurchases shares, or records a net loss.

Ending Equity = Beginning Equity + Net Income − Dividends ± Other Changes
Three-statement link: Net Income from the Income Statement → increases Retained Earnings here. Ending Total Equity here → ties to the Balance Sheet. Dividends paid here → appears in Financing Activities on the Cash Flow Statement.
Components of Equity
1
Common Stock (Par Value) — Nominal value assigned to each share at issuance. Usually immaterial (e.g., $0.01/share).
2
Additional Paid-in Capital (APIC) — Amount received above par value when shares are issued or stock options exercised.
3
Retained Earnings — Cumulative net income since inception, minus all dividends ever paid. This is the most dynamic component.
4
Treasury Stock — Cost of shares the company has repurchased. Shown as a negative (contra-equity) because it reduces equity.
5
Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income (AOCI) — Unrealized gains/losses on certain investments, foreign currency translation, pension adjustments — items not flowing through net income.

Key Metrics & Financial Ratios
Cross-statement calculations — drawing from the P&L, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow Statement
Liquidity ratios measure a company's ability to meet short-term obligations. Higher is generally better — but excessively high ratios may indicate idle assets.
Profitability ratios show how efficiently a company converts revenue or assets into profit. Compare against industry peers and historical trends for context.
Leverage ratios assess debt burden and solvency. High leverage amplifies returns in good times and losses in downturns — context and industry norms matter greatly.
Efficiency ratios reveal how effectively a company manages its assets and working capital cycle. Days metrics are especially important for cash management.
